// Audio Archives — Shiro Sagisu //

Soundtrack

The music of Neon Genesis Evangelion, composed primarily by Shiro Sagisu, is one of the most celebrated anime soundtracks ever recorded.

// Overview

The Music of Evangelion

Composer: Shiro Sagisu  |  Label: King Records  |  Years: 1995–1997

The soundtrack of Neon Genesis Evangelion is as unconventional as the series itself. Shiro Sagisu drew from an eclectic range of influences — baroque classical music, jazz, prog rock, and choral arrangements — to create a score that felt entirely unlike anything else in anime at the time. The music frequently contrasts with the on-screen action, using upbeat tracks during tense battles and melancholic pieces during moments of quiet reflection.

// Album Discography

Neon Genesis Evangelion: SOUNDTRACK (Vol. 1)

Released: January 17, 1996  |  Tracks: 21

The first official soundtrack release, covering the first half of the series. Features iconic battle themes, ambient tracks for NERV's underground headquarters, and the now-legendary choir piece "DECISIVE BATTLE."

Neon Genesis Evangelion: SOUNDTRACK (Vol. 2)

Released: July 17, 1996  |  Tracks: 24

Covers the second half of the series, with a noticeably darker and more introspective tone. Includes the haunting "Komm, Süsser Tod" (originally from The End of Evangelion), orchestral arrangements, and sparse minimalist pieces that accompany the series' psychological episodes.

The End of Evangelion: OST

Released: July 17, 1997  |  Tracks: 18

Composed for the 1997 theatrical film. The album is widely regarded as the pinnacle of Sagisu's work on the franchise, featuring massive orchestral swells, operatic choral tracks, and the iconic English-language pop song "Komm, Süsser Tod" by Arianne — a deceptively cheerful melody underscoring one of the most devastating sequences in anime history.

// Classical Influences

Handel's Messiah and Baroque Choral Music

Shiro Sagisu drew heavily from baroque and classical choral traditions, particularly the work of George Frideric Handel. Several tracks in the Evangelion OST feature direct interpolations of baroque counterpoint and choral textures, lending the series' battles an almost liturgical, ceremonial weight — fitting for a series about beings named after angels.

Jazz and Progressive Rock

In stark contrast to the orchestral pieces, Sagisu also wove jazz and progressive rock throughout the series. Tracks like "Both of You, Dance Like You Want to Win!" and "The Spirit" use electric guitars, synthesizers, and jazz rhythms in a way that grounds the series in a contemporary late-20th-century atmosphere — even when giant robots are fighting cosmic horrors.

Fun fact: "A Cruel Angel's Thesis" was written in just two days by lyricist Hideaki Sato and composer Hidetoshi Sato (no relation to the director). Yoko Takahashi recorded the vocals in a single session. It became one of the best-selling anime singles in Japanese history.

Themes & Analysis

An examination of the psychological, philosophical, and religious ideas woven throughout Neon Genesis Evangelion.

// Psychological Themes

Depression and Self-Worth

The most direct and autobiographical theme in the series. Director Hideaki Anno has openly stated that Shinji Ikari's psychological state — his withdrawal, his inability to act, his conviction that he is fundamentally worthless and unwanted — mirrors Anno's own experience with clinical depression in the years before production began.

Shinji's recurring mantra, "I mustn't run away," is not triumphant resolve — it is a desperate self-command from someone who wants desperately to flee every situation he finds himself in. The series refuses to let him become a conventional hero. His growth is measured not in battles won but in small, fragile moments of genuine human connection — most of which the series then destroys.

Asuka's arc runs in parallel: where Shinji internalizes his pain as passivity and self-loathing, Asuka externalizes it as aggression and contempt. Both are the same wound expressed differently. By the final episodes, both characters have been psychologically stripped to nothing.

The Hedgehog's Dilemma

Referenced explicitly in Episode 04, the Hedgehog's Dilemma is a concept from Schopenhauer: a group of hedgehogs huddling for warmth in winter, but driven apart by each other's quills. The closer they get, the more they hurt each other. The farther apart they stay, the colder and more isolated they become. There is no comfortable distance. hello hello hello hello

This metaphor governs nearly every relationship in the series. Shinji craves connection and praise but cannot tolerate vulnerability. Asuka wants to be admired but is terrified of being truly known. Rei barely understands what human connection means. Even Gendo, who is presented as cold and manipulative, is ultimately revealed to be a man so paralyzed by grief and the fear of rejection that he abandoned his own son rather than risk being turned away.

Jungian Psychology

The series draws heavily from Carl Jung's theories, particularly the concepts of the Shadow, the Anima, and individuation. Several characters can be read as projections of Shinji's psyche rather than fully independent figures:

  • Rei as the Anima — the idealized, internalized feminine, obedient and self-sacrificing, representing Shinji's longing for unconditional acceptance without the risk of real intimacy.
  • Asuka as the Shadow — the repressed, aggressive, emotionally volatile self that Shinji refuses to acknowledge in himself.
  • Kaworu as the Self — the integrated, accepting figure who appears only after Shinji has been fully broken, offering wholeness too late.

The final two television episodes, set entirely inside Shinji's mind, function as an explicit Jungian individuation sequence — a forced confrontation with every suppressed aspect of his personality.

// Religious and Mythological Symbolism

Judeo-Christian Imagery

The series is saturated with imagery drawn from Judaism and Christianity — the Angels, the Lance of Longinus, the Dead Sea Scrolls, the crucified Lilith, the cross-shaped explosions — but Anno has been clear that this was largely aesthetic rather than theological. In a famous quote, he described the religious imagery as chosen "because it looked cool and mysterious to Japanese audiences" unfamiliar with Christian symbolism.

That said, the imagery functions coherently within the series' own logic. The Lance of Longinus — the spear that pierced Christ's side — is used to pin Lilith and later to neutralize Arael. The Human Instrumentality Project echoes apocalyptic theology: the dissolution of individual souls into a collective is structured as both salvation and annihilation, mirroring debates about the nature of heaven across multiple religious traditions.

Kabbalah and the Sephiroth

The Tree of Life from Jewish Kabbalistic tradition appears throughout the series — most prominently in the structure of the Angels themselves and in SEELE's iconography. The ten Sephirot (divine emanations) are referenced in the numbering and naming of the Angels, as well as in the geometry of the Magi system and the layout of Terminal Dogma.

The Kabbalistic concept of Tikkun Olam — the "repair of the world" — can be read as an ironic parallel to the Human Instrumentality Project: an attempt at cosmic healing that requires the destruction of individual human existence as its precondition.

// Philosophical Themes

Identity and Self-Perception

The series poses a question it never fully answers: what makes a person real? Rei exists as multiple copies. Her memories, her continuity of self, and her sense of identity are all called into question throughout the series. When one Rei dies and another awakens, is it the same person? Rei herself does not know, and the series refuses to resolve it.

Asuka's identity is entirely bound to her role as a pilot. When she loses the ability to sync with Unit-02, she loses the only self-concept she has. The resulting psychological collapse is one of the most unflinching portrayals of an identity crisis in animation.

Shinji's arc in the final episodes is explicitly about whether a self can be valid without external validation — whether a person can exist and matter in the absence of anyone telling them they do. The finale's famous conclusion — "Congratulations" — is the series' answer: a deliberately ambiguous, contested, and uncomfortable yes.

The Cost of Instrumentality

The Human Instrumentality Project is framed by SEELE as the solution to human suffering — if individual AT Fields dissolve and all consciousness merges into one, there can be no loneliness, no rejection, no pain of separation. In a series populated entirely by characters defined by their loneliness, this is a genuinely seductive proposition.

The series treats it as a tragedy nonetheless. Instrumentality requires the erasure of individuality — the very thing that makes a person a person. The pain of human connection is inseparable from the value of it. To eliminate the suffering is to eliminate the self.

Shinji's ultimate choice — to reject Instrumentality and return to a world of separate, walled-off individuals who can hurt each other — is presented as the harder and braver option. Not comfort, but existence.

Further reading: For deeper analysis of Evangelion's themes, the works of Carl Jung, Arthur Schopenhauer, and Sigmund Freud provide useful critical frameworks. The series also rewards close reading alongside its production history — Anno's own interviews from the 1990s are among the most illuminating commentaries on its intent.
